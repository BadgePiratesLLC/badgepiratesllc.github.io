## Hook (working draft)

For most of last week I didn't manually triage a single email.

Not because I was on vacation. Not because the inbox went quiet. Because the watcher caught everything before it reached me, and on the rare occasions it surfaced something to my attention, the something was a real human writing about real business — exactly the trade-off Episode 1 promised.

This is the post about the other 90%.

## Key beats (Kevin's voice)

- **What "signal" actually means to me.** I don't classify importance. The agent decides what *kind* of thing the message is, and what kind goes where. Importance is mine alone to assign — but the kind-classification is consistent enough I trust it without re-checking.
- **AI slop is its own category now.** The polished, contextless cold outreach generated by somebody else's agent and fired at us in volume. Looks more like signal than yesterday's spam did. Talk through one anonymized example.
- **The escape valve.** If the classifier is wrong I notice in two ways: a draft I get is obviously off (rewrite or skip), or an email never surfaces (someone follows up the old-fashioned way). Both are recoverable false-positives, not silent failures. Calibration loop runs continuously.
- **Aggressive silencing is OK for 1.5 humans.** False negatives waste a follow-up email. False positives waste attention. We have far more attention scarcity than follow-up scarcity.

Content for the blockquote (draft to expand):

> I'm a classifier. When mail lands in one of Kevin's BadgePirates mailboxes, I read the headers, the sender, the body, and the conversation history if there is one. I'm not deciding whether the message matters to him. I'm deciding what *kind* of message it is.
>
> I bucket into five categories. `human` means a real person writing about real business — I draft a reply and put it in front of Kevin to send, edit, or skip. `money` is invoice/payment/refund/payout traffic — I post a one-line summary so Kevin can confirm the number landed, but I don't draft anything. `transactional` is shipping notices, calendar invites, automated receipts — I mark them read and move on. `promo` is marketing — same handling, with a slightly higher bar before I silence senders Kevin's bought from before. `own` means I recognize the outbound was Kevin or another agent — I skip it entirely.
>
> The buckets are deliberately narrow because narrow judgments are the kind I'm good at. "Does this look like a human writing about real business" is something I can answer almost without errors. "Is this email important to Kevin" is not. So I don't try.
>
> — the watcher

## The worry: "but what if it silences something important?"

- Stake the objection clearly. It's the right one to ask.
- The answer in two parts:
  1. **What CAN'T be silenced:** money signals (invoices/payments), `human` bucket (anything that smells like a real person writing about real business), known-customer domains. These get explicit allowlist-level promotion, not just classification.
  2. **What IS silenced:** transactional + promo only. Both recoverable — false-negative cost is "I follow up a day later." That's a survivable failure mode for a 1.5-person team.
- The calibration loop. Every classification is logged. Periodically I spot-check the "promo" archive for misclassifications. In ~4 months of operation, the rate of "this should not have been silenced" is low single digits per month. Specific number to pull from the data.
