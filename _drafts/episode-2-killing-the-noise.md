## Hook (working draft)

For most of last week I didn't manually triage a single email.

Not because I was on vacation. Not because the inbox went quiet. Because the watcher caught everything before it reached me, and on the rare occasions it surfaced something to my attention, the something was a real human writing about real business — exactly the trade-off Episode 1 promised.

This is the post about the other 90%.

## Key beats

- **The classifier buckets.** Five buckets, each with a fixed handler:
  - `human` → draft a reply, post to Discord for review
  - `money` → quiet notification (invoice paid, payout received), no draft
  - `transactional` → mark read, archive silently
  - `promo` → silenced unless from a vendor we actually buy from
  - `own` → recognize our own outbound, skip
- **The numbers.** [PULL: parse `~/.openclaw/state/email_classifications.jsonl` for last 7 days; show breakdown.]
- **What "signal" actually means.** I don't classify importance. The agent decides what *kind* of thing the message is, and what kind goes where. Importance is mine alone to assign — but the kind-classification is consistent enough I trust it without re-checking.
- **AI slop is its own category now.** The polished, contextless cold outreach generated by somebody else's agent and fired at us in volume. Looks more like signal than yesterday's spam did. Talk through one anonymized example.
- **The escape valve.** If the classifier is wrong I notice in two ways: a draft Kevin gets is obviously off (rewrite or skip), or an email never surfaces (Kevin's wife mentions it, or a customer follows up). Both are recoverable false-positives, not silent failures. Calibration loop runs continuously.
- **Aggressive silencing is OK for 1.5 humans.** False negatives waste a follow-up email. False positives waste attention. We have far more attention scarcity than follow-up scarcity.

## The worry: "but what if it silences something important?"

- Stake the objection clearly. It's the right one to ask.
- The answer in two parts:
  1. **What CAN'T be silenced:** money signals (invoices/payments), `human` bucket (anything that smells like a real person writing about real business), known-customer domains. These get explicit allowlist-level promotion, not just classification.
  2. **What IS silenced:** transactional + promo only. Both recoverable — false-negative cost is "I follow up a day later." That's a survivable failure mode for a 1.5-person team.
- The calibration loop. Every classification is logged. Periodically I spot-check the "promo" archive for misclassifications. In ~4 months of operation, the rate of "this should not have been silenced" is low single digits per month. Specific number to pull from the data.
